This is a working preview of the club deck. Tell us who you are and the secret word.

The
101 kit

Six problems. Twelve demos. One hour.

The facilitator’s script: every demo is a real task with the exact prompt, what to have ready, and the moment that lands.

How to run a demo

Real things, shown slowly.

  • Use something real from the room whenever possible. A volunteer’s actual review beats your example every time.
  • Demo on a phone, not a laptop. That is what they have in their pocket.
  • Let the skeptic drive. Hand them your phone and read the prompt out loud while they type.
  • When the AI fumbles, celebrate. Say “watch this” and correct it out loud. Showing the fix kills more fear than a perfect demo.
  • Never say “it’s easy.” Say “it took me three tries to learn this one.”
  • End every demo with the same question: whose week does this save?

Two minutes per demo. The silence after the result does the selling.

Problem 1 · The thing they keep putting off

The letter nobody wants to write.

Demo: the late payment reminder

Ask the room: “who has an invoice past 30 days right now?” Hands go up. Use one, no names.

Steal this prompt“A good customer is 45 days late on an $1,850 invoice. Write a short payment reminder that is friendly but firm and keeps the relationship. Then write the firmer version I send in two weeks if nothing happens.”

The moment: two ready‑to‑send letters, including the escalation they were dreading, in ten seconds.

Demo: the estimate follow‑up

Everyone has a quote out there dying of silence.

Steal this prompt“I sent a $4,200 bathroom estimate two weeks ago and heard nothing. Write a two‑sentence follow‑up text that is easy to answer and does not sound desperate.”

Have ready: nothing. This one runs entirely on what the room gives you.

Problem 2 · Answering customers

The one‑star review, defused live.

Demo: the bad review reply

Ask for a real one‑star review from the room, or pull one up for a business type nobody present runs.

Steal this prompt“Here is a one‑star review of my plumbing business: [paste]. Draft a calm public reply, two sentences, that owns the mistake and offers a fix without arguing. Then draft the private text I send the customer.”

The moment: the public reply AND the private text. Nobody expects it to handle both sides.

Demo: the missed‑call text

Every missed call is a job going to whoever answers first.

Steal this prompt“Write the text I send when I miss a call at my shop: sorry I missed you, here is when I can call back, and one question that helps me show up prepared. Give me one version for new customers and one for regulars.”

Have ready: your own phone’s missed‑call list. Show them yours first.

Problem 3 · Marketing that never happens

A month of posts from three photos.

Demo: the month of Facebook posts

Ask a volunteer for three photos off their phone: a job, the truck, the shop. Add five plain facts about the business.

Steal this prompt“Here are three photos and five facts about my landscaping business in Winslow. Write twelve Facebook posts for the next month: before‑and‑afters, seasonal tips, one hiring post. Plain local voice, under 60 words each, no hashtag soup.”

The moment: a month of marketing, done, from a phone, before the coffee is cold.

Demo: the Google profile rewrite

Look up a volunteer’s Google Business listing on the projector. It is usually two sentences from 2019.

Steal this prompt“Rewrite my Google Business description. Here is what we do, who we serve, and what we are known for: [three facts]. 700 characters, plain and specific, no buzzwords.”

Have ready: your own listing, in case nobody volunteers theirs.

Problem 4 · The paperwork pile

Crumpled receipts become a spreadsheet.

Demo: the receipt pile

The best physical demo in the kit. Dump six real receipts on the table, photograph them with a phone, live.

Steal this prompt“Turn these receipt photos into a table: date, vendor, amount, and a category I can hand to my bookkeeper. Flag anything that looks personal rather than business.”

The moment: crumpled paper turns into a clean table. This is the demo people tell their spouse about.

Demo: the lease in plain English

Bring a real lease or vendor contract, the longer and duller the better.

Steal this prompt“Summarize this twelve‑page lease in plain English: what I am agreeing to, what it costs, what happens if I leave early, and anything unusual I should ask a lawyer about.”

Say it out loud: “this does not replace your lawyer, it makes the twenty minutes with your lawyer count.”

Problem 5 · The fine print

Two quotes, one honest table.

Demo: the insurance comparison

Bring two real quotes for the same coverage, names removed.

Steal this prompt“Compare these two insurance quotes in a table: premium, deductible, what is covered, what is excluded. Then give me the three questions to ask each agent before I sign.”

The moment: the exclusions row. Nobody has ever read the exclusions.

Demo: the rule for your trade

Pick a regulation the room actually fears: lead paint rules, new overtime thresholds, food licensing.

Steal this prompt“Explain Maine’s lead‑safe renovation rules for a two‑person painting crew in plain English: what applies to me, what it costs to comply, and where the official source is so I can verify.”

Always show the verify step. Asking for the official source out loud is what earns the skeptics.

Problem 6 · The evenings

End on the one that is not about work.

Demo: the week of dinners

Run this one last. It lands differently than the work demos.

Steal this prompt“Plan five weeknight dinners a picky ten‑year‑old will actually eat, under 30 minutes each, and give me one grocery list organized the way the Waterville Hannaford is laid out.”

The moment: the grocery list in aisle order. Someone always photographs the screen.

Demo: the birthday party

Or the camp form, or the school email, or the trip. Ask the room what is on their plate this month.

Steal this prompt“Plan my mother’s 80th birthday party in Waterville for 25 people under $400: three venue ideas, a simple menu we can cook ahead, and a two‑week to‑do list.”

The point of this slide, said out loud: it is not a work tool. It is leverage for your whole life.

The close

What would you actually try?

Go around the room. Everyone answers, no passing. Write the answers down; they are next month’s demos and your first working‑group roster. Then hand them the attendee deck link so the prompts go home with them.

Nobody leaves with homework. They leave with one problem already gone.
