THE AI HOMEBREW CLUB

A modern revival of the Homebrew Computer Club (1975–1986) — the garage meetup that gave the world the personal computer. It was never really about the machines. It was about people learning, together, to hold power over a new technology. You don't need to be an engineer here. You need to be curious.

origins

Menlo Park garage → SLAC auditorium → the personal computer industry

The Homebrew Computer Club held its first meeting in March 1975 in Gordon French's garage in Menlo Park, California — sparked by the MITS Altair 8800 appearing on the cover of Popular Electronics. Co-founded by French and Fred Moore, it quickly outgrew the garage and settled into the auditorium at SLAC (Stanford Linear Accelerator Center), meeting every two weeks.

Steve Wozniak attended the very first meeting and called it one of the most important nights of his life. The Apple I existed largely because he wanted something to show off there — and when he did, he handed out photocopies of his schematics for free.

the tenets

Mostly unwritten — but fiercely held

TENET_01

"Give to help others."

The closest thing to an official motto, pushed by co-founder Fred Moore. You shared what you knew, freely — schematics, code listings, chip sources, workarounds. Knowledge flowed outward; it was never hoarded for advantage.

TENET_02

Radical openness of information

Members passed around paper tapes of software and photocopied designs. This ethos is literally why Bill Gates wrote his 1976 "Open Letter to Hobbyists" complaining that Homebrew members were copying Altair BASIC without paying. The club treated software like folklore, not property.

TENET_03

Show and tell over lecture

Moderator Lee Felsenstein ran two signature segments: the "mapping" period, where anyone could stand up and announce what they were working on, needed, or had to give away; and the "random access" period, where people physically clustered around whoever mentioned something interesting. Demos beat presentations. Working hardware beat vaporware.

TENET_04

No gatekeeping, no hierarchy

No membership requirements, no dues that mattered, no credentials checked at the door. Hardcore engineers sat next to teenagers and curious dabblers. Felsenstein ran meetings with a pointer stick and deliberate irreverence — "moderating by insult" — specifically to keep any one person or vendor from dominating.

TENET_05

Hands-on empowerment as ideology

Not just a hobby club — it carried a countercultural conviction (inherited from the People's Computer Company and Ted Nelson's Computer Lib) that ordinary people should own and control computing power, which at the time belonged to corporations and institutions. "Computers for the people" was the point.

TENET_06

Commerce grew out of community — not the reverse

More than 20 companies trace their roots to the club, Apple most famously. But you came to share, and business happened as a side effect. Selling was tolerated; pitching was gauche.

TENET_07

A newsletter as connective tissue

The club newsletter spread ideas well beyond the room and is credited with helping seed the entire personal computer industry's culture.

"It was the most important night of my life... I was so shy, I couldn't have raised my hand, but I went home and started designing what became the Apple I." — Steve Wozniak, on the first Homebrew meeting

how a meeting runs

Borrowed from Lee Felsenstein, who kept a room of 400 strangers feeling like a garage

SegmentWhat happened
MAPPING Open mic, rapid-fire. Anyone stands up: what you're building, what you need, what you're giving away. No slides, no pitches, ~1 minute each.
RANDOM ACCESS Unstructured clustering. You physically walk over to whoever said something interesting. Deals, collaborations, and companies formed here.
DEMOS Working hardware on folding tables. If it ran, people gathered. If it didn't, you came back in two weeks.

the program

Two tracks, one room — the technical and the human, deliberately mixed

Homebrew looked like a hardware club, but its real curriculum was human: confidence, generosity, and judgment about a technology that was about to change everyone's life. Ours is the same. Half of what we do is hands-on with AI. The other half is about us — how we work, decide, and stay ourselves while machines get smarter. Every meeting deliberately mixes both, because a room of only builders or only talkers misses the point.

TRACK_A · WORKING WITH AGENTS

The technical side — no degree required

Managing AI agents is the new literacy, and it looks less like programming than like managing people: giving clear instructions, delegating the right-sized task, reviewing the work instead of trusting it blindly, and knowing when to step in. We practice this live — members bring real agents doing real work (research, bookkeeping, writing, home projects) and show how they direct them, where they fail, and what they've learned to never let them do unsupervised. If you can brief a contractor, you can run an agent.

TRACK_B · STAYING HUMAN

The human side — judgment, honesty, and each other

The questions that matter here aren't technical: What am I still responsible for when an agent did the work? What should I refuse to automate? What happened to my job, my craft, my kid's homework this month? We hold open, no-judgment conversation on this every session — wins and failures. The rule is the same as 1975: nobody gets talked down to, and the person with no technical background often asks the most important question in the room.

THE_BRIDGE

Every demo answers a human question

Our format forces the bridge: every technical demo ends with "what did this change for you?" — time back, money saved, a worry created, a skill atrophying. And every human discussion ends with "what would you actually try?" so it turns into next meeting's demo. Tech without the human story is showing off; the human story without practice is just worrying out loud. The club exists to keep the loop closed.

getting started

Zero to your first demo — no experience required

In 1975 the price of admission was a soldering iron. In 2026 it's a free code editor. You don't need to be a programmer — Visual Studio Code is just the workbench where modern AI agents live and work. Install it once, and you're ready for everything we do at the club.

STEP_01

Install Visual Studio Code

Free, from Microsoft, runs on Windows, Mac, and Linux. Download it at code.visualstudio.com, or just follow along with the walkthrough below — it takes about two minutes.

STEP_02

Install Claude Code

Now add the agent itself. Open the terminal inside VS Code with Ctrl+` (the backtick key, under Esc), make sure it's PowerShell (the prompt starts with PS), and run:

irm https://claude.ai/install.ps1 | iex

When it finishes, close VS Code completely and reopen it so the terminal picks up the new PATH. Then type claude in the terminal to start it.

STEP_03

Come to a meeting

You don't need a working setup to show up — curiosity is the only requirement. Stand up during the mapping period and say what you want to learn. By the next meeting, you'll have something to demo.
